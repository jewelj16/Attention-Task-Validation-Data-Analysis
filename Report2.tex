\section{Introduction}
% ===========================================================================

\subsection{Background and Motivation}

Selective attention is a core cognitive function \citep{treisman1980feature}.
Laboratory visual-search paradigms are the gold standard for measuring it,
but gamified mobile assessments offer a scalable alternative
\citep{lumsden2016gamification} that requires validation.

This report documents the statistical inference for our $2 \times 2$ mixed
factorial study validating a mobile game-based modality against a
laboratory modality under different target loads (single vs.\
multiple). We applied mixed ANOVA, non-parametric
methods, and post-hoc tests to evaluate our six
hypotheses.

\subsection{Research Hypotheses}

\begin{description}
  \item[H1 --- Concurrent Validity] \hfill \\
    $H_0$: There is no correlation between performance on the Game and Lab task ($r = 0$). \\
    $H_1$: Performance on the Game task is positively correlated with performance on the Lab task ($r > 0$, one-tailed).
  \item[H2 --- Load Effect] \hfill \\
    $H_0$: RT and accuracy do not differ between the Single Target and Multiple Target conditions ($\mu_{\text{single}} = \mu_{\text{multiple}}$). \\
    $H_1$: The Multiple Target condition results in slower RTs and lower accuracies compared to the Single Target condition, reflecting increased attentional load ($\mu_{\text{multiple}} > \mu_{\text{single}}$ for RT; $\mu_{\text{multiple}} < \mu_{\text{single}}$ for accuracy, one-tailed).
  \item[H3 --- Modality Effect] \hfill \\
    $H_0$: There is no systematic difference in performance between the Lab and Game interfaces ($\mu_{\text{lab}} = \mu_{\text{game}}$). \\
    $H_1$: Performance differs systematically between the Lab and Game interfaces ($\mu_{\text{lab}} \neq \mu_{\text{game}}$, two-tailed).
  \item[H4 --- Level Effect] \hfill \\
    $H_0$: Performance is stable across game levels, with no linear relationship between level and RT ($r = 0$). \\
    $H_1$: RT changes systematically across game levels ($r \neq 0$, two-tailed).
  \item[H5 --- Target Colour Effect] \hfill \\
    $H_0$: Target colour has no effect on RT or accuracy ($\mu_{\text{red}} = \mu_{\text{other}}$). \\
    $H_1$: Red targets result in faster RTs and higher accuracy compared to other colours, consistent with Feature Integration Theory \citep{treisman1980feature} ($\mu_{\text{red}} < \mu_{\text{other}}$ for RT; $\mu_{\text{red}} > \mu_{\text{other}}$ for accuracy, one-tailed).
  \item[H6 --- Interaction Effect] \hfill \\
    $H_0$: The magnitude of the modality penalty does not differ between target load groups. \\
    $H_1$: The magnitude of the modality penalty differs between the single-target and multiple-target groups (two-tailed).
\end{description}

\section{Methods}
% ===========================================================================

\subsection{Participants and Design}

$N = 37$ participants were randomly assigned to one of two Target Load
groups. To control for practice and fatigue effects, the order of modalities
(Lab first or Game first) was counterbalanced within each group.

    \toprule
    \textbf{Group} & \textbf{Condition} & \textbf{n} & \textbf{IDs} \\
    \midrule
    Group A & Single Target & 21 & 1--21 \\
    Group B & Multiple Target & 16 & 22--37 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\subsection{Measures}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Reaction Time (RT):} Reaction Time (RT) per participant, in seconds. For laboratory trials the raw onset-to-response
For game trials, millisecond timestamps were converted to
    seconds.
  \item \textbf{Accuracy:} Proportion of trials on which the participant
    correctly identified the target.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{Assumption Checks and Data Transformation}

\subsubsection{Normality}

Shapiro--Wilk tests applied to raw RT means revealed violations of
normality in several condition cells.
To restore approximate normality a natural-log transformation was applied:
\[
  \log\text{RT} = \ln(\text{RT}_{\text{mean}})
\]
Post-transformation Shapiro–Wilk tests revealed that while normality was restored for the multiple target load ($p = .296$), the single target load condition still departed significantly from normality ($p = .0045$).

\subsubsection{Homogeneity of Variance}

Levene’s test applied to RT across condition cells returned a significant result ($p < .001$), indicating that the assumption of homogeneity of variance was violated.

\subsection{Statistical Analyses}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Mixed ANOVA (RT):} A $2 \times 2$ mixed ANOVA on raw (untransformed) RT
    tested the main effects of target load and modality, and their
    interaction. $\eta^2_p$ is reported as the effect-size estimate.
  \item \textbf{Post-hoc paired $t$-tests:} Following a significant
    interaction, paired $t$-tests compared lab and game RT within each
    target-load group separately, with Cohen's $d$ as the effect size.
  \item \textbf{Non-parametric corroboration:}
    Mann--Whitney $U$ tests (between-subjects comparisons) and Wilcoxon
    signed-rank tests (within-subjects comparisons) were used to corroborate
    the ANOVA findings.
  \item \textbf{Accuracy tests:} Mann--Whitney $U$ and Wilcoxon signed-rank
    tests assessed load and modality effects on accuracy, given that
    proportions rarely satisfy normality assumptions.
  \item \textbf{Concurrent Validity (H1):} Pearson correlation between
    per-participant lab RT and game RT.
  \item \textbf{Level Effect (H4):} Pearson correlation between game-level
    number and mean RT across levels.
  \item \textbf{Colour Effect (H5):} Mann--Whitney $U$ test comparing
    participant-mean RT for red vs.\ non-red targets in the lab modality.
\end{enumerate}

All tests were conducted two-tailed at $\alpha = .05$.
Bayes Factors (BF$_{10}$) are reported alongside frequentist $p$-values where
available, providing a continuous measure of evidential strength.

  \label{fig:h2h3h6}

\paragraph{Main effect of target load (H2).}
$F(1, 35) = 32.23$, $p < .001$.
Multiple-target trials were significantly faster than single-target trials,
refuting H2.

\paragraph{Main effect of modality (H3).}
$F(1, 35) = 117.97$, $p < .001$.
Game RTs were significantly longer than lab RTs across both load conditions,
supporting H3.

\subsection{H6: Load $\times$ Modality Interaction}

The significant interaction, $F(1, 35) = 40.34$, $p < .001$, indicates
that the magnitude of the modality penalty differed between the two load
conditions.
To unpack this interaction, separate paired $t$-tests were conducted
comparing lab and game RT within each target-load group.

\subsubsection{Post-hoc Paired \textit{t}-tests}

\paragraph{Single-target group.}
$t(20) = -11.73$, $p < .001$, Cohen's $d = 3.42$,
95\% CI $[-1.89, -1.32]$, BF$_{10} = 4.777 \times 10^7$.
The game penalty in the single-target group is extremely large
($d = 3.42$), indicating that participants were dramatically slower on the
game platform relative to the lab when tracking only one target.

\paragraph{Multiple-target group.}
$t(15) = -2.55$, $p = .022$, Cohen's $d = 0.87$,
95\% CI $[-0.64, -0.06]$, BF$_{10} = 2.87$.
Although the game penalty remains statistically significant in the
multiple-target group, its magnitude is substantially smaller ($d = 0.87$)
than in the single-target group.

These results reveal a counterintuitive pattern: the game interface
imposes a \emph{proportionally greater penalty} when the task is cognitively
simpler (single target).
One plausible explanation is that the single-target search is rapid and
automatic in the lab (feature search under Feature Integration Theory),
whereas the game's unfamiliar interface disrupts this automaticity,
disproportionately slowing single-target responses.
Under multiple-target load, both lab and game conditions require slower,
conjunction-based search, attenuating the relative game penalty.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{{BRSM plots/download (7)}.png}
  \caption{H6: Non-linear interface penalty. Individual participant data
           points (jittered) with group means and 95\% CI. The game penalty
           is massive in the single-target condition (Cohen's $d = 3.42$)
           but substantially reduced for multiple targets ($d = 0.87$).}
  \label{fig:h6_interaction}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Non-Parametric Corroboration}

To confirm that the ANOVA findings on RTs generalise to raw (untransformed)
RT, non-parametric tests were applied to the original data.

\subsubsection{Reaction Time}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Load effect (Mann--Whitney $U$):}
    $U = 914.0$, $p = .008$, RBC $= 0.36$, CLES $= 0.68$.
    Corroborates the significant load effect detected by the ANOVA.
  \item \textbf{Modality effect (Wilcoxon signed-rank):}
    $W = 24.0$, $p < .001$, RBC $= -0.93$, CLES $= 0.11$.
    A large rank-biserial correlation ($|$RBC$| = 0.93$) confirms a
    strong, systematic modality effect consistent with the ANOVA.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Accuracy}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Load effect on accuracy (Mann--Whitney $U$):}
    $U = 1009.0$, $p < .001$, RBC $= 0.50$, CLES $= 0.75$.
    Multiple-target conditions produced significantly lower accuracy.
  \item \textbf{Modality effect on accuracy (Wilcoxon signed-rank):}
    $W = 50.0$, $p = .014$, RBC $= 0.60$, CLES $= 0.68$.
    Game accuracy was significantly lower than lab accuracy.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{H1: Concurrent Validity}

A Pearson correlation between per-participant lab RT and game RT (across all
conditions) yielded $r = 0.076$, $p = .656$.
This result does \emph{not} support H1.
The near-zero correlation indicates that individual differences in lab RT
do not predict individual differences in game RT.
A possible explanation is that the game interface introduces large,
participant-specific learning or adaptation effects that overwhelm any
underlying attentional trait shared across platforms.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.72\textwidth]{{BRSM plots/download (3)}.png}
  \caption{H1: Concurrent validity scatter plot. Each point is one
           participant's mean lab RT vs.\ mean game RT. The flat regression
           line ($r = 0.076$, $p = .656$) confirms no significant
           concurrent validity relationship between the two platforms.}
  \label{fig:concurrent_validity}
\end{figure}

\subsection{H4: Level Effect}

Within the game modality, the Pearson correlation between level number and
mean RT across levels was $r = 0.802$, $p < .001$.
This strong positive correlation supports H4: reaction time increased
steadily across sequential game levels, consistent with fatigue accumulation
or rising task difficulty as levels progressed.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{{BRSM plots/download (5)}.png}
  \caption{H4: Level effect in the game modality. Mean RT per level (teal
           line with 95\% CI shading) rises across 15 game
           levels. The dashed red regression line confirms a strong positive
           trend ($r = 0.802$, $p < .001$).}
  \label{fig:level_effect}
\end{figure}

\subsection{H5: Target Colour Effect}

A Mann--Whitney $U$ test comparing participant-mean RT for red versus
non-red targets in the lab modality returned $U = 395.0$, $p = .002$,
RBC $= -0.42$.
Red targets were associated with faster responses than non-red targets,
supporting H5 and consistent with the colour-salience prediction from
Feature Integration Theory: a uniquely coloured target ``pops out'' from
a display of differently coloured distractors, enabling faster feature
search.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{{BRSM plots/download (6)}.png}
  \caption{H5: Target colour effect in the lab modality. Red targets
           (left) produced faster RTs than other-coloured targets (right),
           consistent with a colour pop-out (feature search) advantage
           ($U = 395.0$, $p = .002$).}
  \label{fig:colour_effect}
\end{figure}

\section{Conclusion}
% ===========================================================================

Table~\ref{tab:summary} provides a consolidated overview of all hypothesis
tests and their outcomes.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \caption{Summary of hypothesis tests and conclusions.}
  \label{tab:summary}
  \setlength{\tabcolsep}{4pt}
  \begin{tabular}{p{3.2cm} p{3.5cm} p{3.5cm} p{4.2cm}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Hypothesis} & \textbf{Test} & \textbf{Result} &
      \textbf{Conclusion} \\
    \midrule
    H1: Concurrent Validity &
      Pearson $r$ &
      $r = 0.076$, $p = .656$ &
      \textbf{Not supported.}  No significant correlation. \\[4pt]

    H2: Load Effect &
      $2 \times 2$ Mixed ANOVA &
      $F(1,35) = 32.23$, $p < .001$ &
      \textbf{Refuted.}  Multiple targets resulted in faster RTs. \\[4pt]

    H3: Modality Effect &
      $2 \times 2$ Mixed ANOVA &
      $F(1,35) = 117.97$, $p < .001$ &
      \textbf{Supported.}  Game interface slows RT. \\[4pt]

    H4: Level Effect &
      Pearson $r$ &
      $r = 0.802$, $p < .001$ &
      \textbf{Supported.}  RT rises across game levels. \\[4pt]

    H5: Target Colour &
      Mann--Whitney $U$ &
      $U = 395.0$, $p = .002$ &
      \textbf{Supported.}  Red targets detected faster. \\[4pt]

    H6: Interaction Effect &
      $2 \times 2$ Mixed ANOVA &
      $F(1,35) = 40.34$, $p < .001$ &
      \textbf{Supported.}  Game penalty is disproportionately
      larger for single-target ($d = 3.42$) than for
      multiple-target ($d = 0.87$) conditions. \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

This Phase~2 analysis confirms significant modality (H3) and interaction (H6)
effects: the game interface systematically slows reaction times, particularly
during single-target feature search. However, the load effect hypothesis (H2)
was refuted, as multiple-target trials unexpectedly yielded faster responses.
Non-parametric tests corroborated these findings. Furthermore, target colour
salience (H5) and game-level fatigue (H4) significantly impacted performance,
serving as important methodological cautions.

Critically, lab and game performance were uncorrelated, failing to demonstrate
concurrent validity (H1). This challenges the game's viability as a direct
drop-in replacement for laboratory measures. Future research should explore
whether familiarisation training, adaptive levelling, or interface redesigns
can bridge this validity gap.
